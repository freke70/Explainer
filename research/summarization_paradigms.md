## What separates an excellent summary from a mediocre one

In discourse comprehension theory, summarizing is not only deleting; it is applying operations that produce a **macrostructure** (gist-level representation). ### Hierarchical organization and relational density without overload

A useful design tension is:

- **Density** supports expert-like retrieval pathways (many routes to the concept) but risks overload for novices.
- **Clarity** protects working memory but can become shallow if it reduces knowledge to disconnected bullet points.

In practice, many excellent summaries maintain **two coupled layers**:

## Summarization frameworks and when each is cognitively appropriate

## Connections, structure, and metacognition

### Relational and systems thinking: why connections change retrieval and transfer

Expertise research shows that experts organize knowledge by deep structure. ## Discipline differences and common failure modes

### What adapts across disciplines and what stays universal

Some principles appear broadly universal across domains:

- **Working memory is limited**, so summaries need chunking and signaling. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn3search1Ņłéturn1search0Ņłü  
- **Generation and elaboration improve learning** when they trigger constructive processing. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn0search2Ņłéturn7search13Ņłéturn0search3Ņłü  
- **Retrieval practice and spacing** are reliably powerful for long-term retention. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn2search2Ņłéturn9search1Ņłéturn0search0Ņłü  
- **Misleading fluency** drives poor study choices; summaries should be designed to defeat fluency illusions, not reinforce them. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn12search3Ņłéturn18search0Ņłü
