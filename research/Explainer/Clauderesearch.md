# How understanding actually happens in the listening mind

**Genuine understanding occurs when the brain's predictive model fails productively — not when information flows smoothly.** This is the central, counterintuitive finding that emerges from synthesizing research across cognitive science, educational psychology, discourse comprehension, and expertise studies. The implication overturns conventional explanation wisdom: the optimal spoken explanation is not one that makes everything maximally clear, but one that engineers precisely calibrated prediction errors at exactly the moments where the listener's mental model needs restructuring. What follows maps the cognitive machinery underlying extended spoken explanation — from the neural signature of insight to the dynamics of prior knowledge resistance — organized around mechanisms rather than prescriptions, with specific attention to actionable findings and genuine unknown unknowns.

---

## Understanding is a discrete restructuring event, not gradual accumulation

The moment of genuine understanding has a neural signature. Mark Beeman and John Kounios (2004) identified a **gamma-band burst (~40 Hz) in the right anterior superior temporal gyrus** approximately 300 milliseconds before a person signals an insight solution. This region specializes in making connections across semantically distant information — integrating meanings that the left hemisphere's more focused processing misses. Critically, this burst is preceded by **alpha-band suppression over visual cortex** roughly 1.5 seconds earlier, a sensory-gating mechanism that shields fragile internal processing from external interference. The brain literally turns down the outside world to protect the emerging insight.

This fits Knoblich and Ohlsson's Representational Change Theory (1999), which identifies two mechanisms enabling the "click" of understanding. **Constraint relaxation** involves releasing self-imposed assumptions about how a domain works — assumptions that feel so natural they're invisible. **Chunk decomposition** means breaking apart familiar perceptual groupings into components that can be recombined. Both processes explain why insight often feels sudden: the solver has been trapped by an inappropriate representation, and understanding arrives not through accumulation but through restructuring the representation itself. Knoblich demonstrated this elegantly with matchstick arithmetic problems: problems requiring changes to deeply assumed constraints (operators rather than values) were dramatically harder, and eye-tracking confirmed solvers fixated on values rather than operators, blind to their own representational bias.

A striking finding from Reverberi et al. (2005) makes the mechanism concrete: patients with lateral prefrontal cortex damage solved **82% of the hardest insight problems versus 43% for healthy controls**. The prefrontal cortex — seat of executive control — actively imposes the very constraints that block restructuring. This suggests that understanding sometimes requires relaxing cognitive control, not intensifying it.

Can this be trained? Ahmed and Patrick (2015) developed an "inconsistency identification" procedure: training people to identify mismatches between what they assume a problem says and what it actually says. This significantly improved insight problem-solving rates across diverse problems, with effects sustained after 48 hours. The improvement came not from awareness of problem types but from the meta-skill of questioning one's own representation.

Conditions that facilitate insight converge on a coherent picture: **positive mood** broadens semantic activation and enhances sensitivity to distant associations (Subramaniam et al., 2009); **incubation** releases solvers from the grip of false organizing assumptions (Sio & Ormerod, 2009 meta-analysis); **internally focused attention** shields fragile processing from external interference. Anxiety specifically inhibits insight while potentially enhancing analytical problem-solving — a dissociation with direct implications for how explanation environments should feel. Verbal overshadowing (Schooler, Ohlsson & Brooks, 1993) is particularly relevant: thinking aloud during insight problems reduces solution rates, because language forces analytical processing that suppresses the associative processing insight requires. This suggests that during the most critical moments of an explanation — where the listener needs to restructure their model — the explainer should create silence, not fill it.

## The brain learns from failed predictions, not confirmed ones

A finding from predictive processing research reframes everything about explanation design. Under Karl Friston's and Andy Clark's framework, the brain is a hierarchical prediction machine that continuously generates top-down expectations about incoming information. **Learning occurs specifically when these predictions fail** — when prediction error propagates upward through the cortical hierarchy to update the internal generative model. Without targeted prediction error, there is no targeted learning.

This connects to Jeffrey Zacks's Event Segmentation Theory in a way that has not been widely appreciated in explanation design. The brain automatically parses continuous experience into discrete events maintained in working memory. When prediction error spikes — through changes in actors, locations, goals, or causal structure — an **event boundary** fires, flushing and rebuilding the current event model. Swallow, Zacks, and Abrams (2009) found that **information present at event boundaries is remembered better than information within events**. People who segment experience hierarchically (at both fine and coarse timescales) show superior memory, possibly because hierarchical encoding facilitates deeper processing.

The implication is architectural: an explainer should deliberately engineer event boundaries through shifts in topic, perspective, or framing, and place the most important takeaways at or immediately after these transitions. Simultaneously, connected ideas that should be processed as a unit need explicit continuity signals to prevent unwanted segmentation.

Three other prediction-error phenomena converge on the same principle:

The **hypercorrection effect** (Butterfield & Metcalfe, 2001) shows that high-confidence errors are corrected at **70–90% rates versus 40–50% for low-confidence errors** after feedback. When you are confident and wrong, the metacognitive mismatch captures attention and enhances encoding — confirmed by ERP data showing a P300 late positivity during corrective feedback for high-confidence errors. This means an explainer should deliberately surface confident misconceptions, invite the audience to commit to them, and then deliver the correction. The surprise of being wrong about something you were sure about creates the most powerful encoding moment available.

The **pretesting effect** (Richland, Kornell & Kao, 2009) shows that testing people on material before they learn it — even when they answer virtually everything incorrectly — enhances subsequent learning with an effect size up to **d = 1.1**. The mechanism involves semantic activation of relevant networks plus creation of a prediction against which subsequent information generates productive error. Pan et al. (2020) demonstrated that pretesting reduced mind wandering and enhanced learning from online lectures — a finding directly applicable to explanation design.

The **PACE framework** (Gruber & Ranganath, 2019) ties curiosity itself to prediction error: curiosity is triggered by significant prediction errors appraised as indicators of valuable future information. Critically, Gruber, Gelman, and Ranganath (2014) discovered that curiosity-driven dopamine release enhances hippocampus-dependent memory not only for the target of curiosity **but for all incidental information encountered during the curious state** — even across a 24-hour delay. Triggering curiosity before key content creates a neurochemical window of enhanced encoding for everything that follows.

## Analogies work through structural alignment but fail through surface seduction

Dedre Gentner's Structure-Mapping Theory (1983) remains the most rigorously validated account of how analogies produce understanding. The core mechanism: analogies map **relational structure** (relations between objects), not object attributes, from a familiar base domain to an unfamiliar target. The systematicity principle specifies that people prefer to map interconnected systems of higher-order relations — causal chains, mechanisms — over isolated matching facts. This is why "electricity is like flowing water" works: the relational system (pressure causes flow through conduits meeting resistance) transfers, while the objects themselves (wires vs. pipes) are deliberately mismatched.

The most important practical finding is the **retrieval-mapping gap**. Gentner et al. (1993) and Gick and Holyoak (1980) established that retrieval from long-term memory is dominated by surface similarity — what looks alike gets pulled from memory — while mapping (once two items are in working memory) is dominated by structural similarity. This explains a fundamental puzzle: analogies are easy to see once pointed out but hard to spontaneously generate. The MAC/FAC model (Forbus, Gentner & Law, 1995) captures the computational logic: a fast surface-based initial screen retrieves candidates, then structural mapping evaluates them. For explanation design, this means analogies must be explicitly provided; relying on listeners to spontaneously retrieve structurally appropriate analogies is unreliable.

Holyoak and Thagard's multi-constraint theory (1989) adds two additional pressures beyond structure: **semantic similarity** between corresponding elements facilitates mapping, and **pragmatic centrality** favors mappings involving elements the reasoner considers important for their current goal. These "soft" constraints interact through parallel constraint satisfaction — they usually converge but sometimes conflict, and the system finds the best overall fit.

When do analogies backfire? The research identifies four failure modes. First, when the base domain is poorly understood, there is no systematic structure to map. Second, when surface similarity competes with structural alignment, especially for novices who default to surface matches (Gentner & Toupin, 1986). Third, when learners overextend the analogy beyond its valid structural mapping. Spiro et al. (1989) documented "reductive analogy" in biomedical education: medical students reduced complex target concepts to just those aspects covered by the source analogy, generating entrenched misconceptions. The rowing-crew analogy for muscle fiber contraction led to persistent errors about force production because students treated the analogy as exhaustive.

The solution for complex concepts is **multiple analogies** covering different aspects, combined with explicit marking of where each analogy breaks down. Spiro's Cognitive Flexibility Theory prescribes "criss-crossing the conceptual landscape" through multiple perspectives and cases. For simpler concepts, Clement's (1993) **bridging analogies** use chains of intermediate cases to connect familiar anchors to unfamiliar targets — demonstrated powerfully in physics education where students who initially rejected that a table exerts upward force on a book were brought to acceptance through a chain: hand on spring → book on foam pad → book on flexible board → book on rigid table. Each link was plausible; the chain traversed the conceptual distance in manageable steps.

**Concreteness fading** (Goldstone & Son, 2005) resolves the tension between concrete and abstract: begin with concrete, richly grounded representations that provide accessibility, then systematically strip away surface features while preserving relational structure. This three-step progression (concrete → iconic → abstract) outperformed all other orderings in Goldstone and Son's experiments on competitive specialization, and was confirmed by McNeil and Fyfe (2012) with effects persisting at three-week delays. But Kaminski, Sloutsky, and Heckler (2008) showed that when concrete examples carry too much extraneous perceptual information, they can **block abstraction and transfer entirely** — even multiple concrete examples plus a generic example could not match a single abstract example. The resolution is not "concrete vs. abstract" but alignment: concrete features should highlight, not distract from, the target relational structure.

## Prior knowledge is an active combatant, not a passive substrate

The most important finding in conceptual change research is how rarely it actually happens. Chinn and Brewer's (1993) taxonomy identifies **eight distinct responses** to anomalous data — ignoring, rejecting, professing uncertainty, excluding, holding in abeyance, reinterpreting, making peripheral changes, and genuine theory change — of which **only the last constitutes actual conceptual change**. Responses 1 through 6 protect the existing theory entirely. Response 7 makes only surface modifications. Research consistently shows that genuine theory change is the least common response.

Michelene Chi's (2008) framework explains why some misconceptions are far more resistant than others by distinguishing three grain sizes of conflict. **Individual false beliefs** (smallest grain) can be corrected through direct refutation. **Flawed mental models** require transforming multiple interconnected beliefs — Chi demonstrated about 60% of learners can achieve this with appropriate instruction. **Ontological category shifts** — the most radical form — require moving a concept from one fundamental category to another (e.g., from "matter" to "process"). Students systematically miscategorize emergent processes like heat transfer, diffusion, and natural selection as direct causal processes or substances. Because ontological categories carry mutually exclusive properties, shifting between them is extraordinarily difficult. Learners often lack both awareness that a shift is needed and an alternative category to shift into.

Vosniadou's framework theory (1994) adds a crucial mechanism: **synthetic models**. When learners attempt to assimilate scientific information while retaining their framework presuppositions, they create intermediate models that blend old and new — children's hollow-sphere model of Earth (people live on a flat surface inside a sphere) is the classic example. These synthetic models feel coherent to the learner, creating the dangerous illusion of successful understanding.

Andrea diSessa's competing knowledge-in-pieces framework (1993) offers a productively different perspective. Rather than viewing prior knowledge as coherent theories to be replaced, diSessa proposes **p-prims** (phenomenological primitives) — small, intuitive knowledge elements like "closer is stronger" or "force as mover" that are neither correct nor incorrect in themselves but can be appropriately or inappropriately activated in context. This matters enormously for explanation design: instead of confronting and replacing "misconceptions," an explainer can **cue productive p-prims** and help learners understand the boundary conditions of each intuition's applicability. Since p-prims persist across contexts, refuting one in a specific context won't eliminate it — it will resurface elsewhere.

The **backfire effect** — where corrections actually strengthen the misconception — turns out to be **far less robust than widely believed**. Wood and Porter (2019) conducted the largest replication with 8,100 participants across 36 issues and found evidence of backfire on only one issue. Nyhan himself (2021, PNAS) acknowledged the scientific literature does not support backfire as a general phenomenon. However, motivated reasoning and confirmation bias remain robust, and Chinn and Brewer's non-change responses are pervasive. The practical implication: providing correct information typically produces modest improvements in accuracy, but the challenge is ensuring information reaches the situation model level rather than being superficially accommodated.

Meyer and Land's (2003) **threshold concepts** identify certain ideas as transformative portals — once grasped, they irreversibly change how a learner sees an entire domain, integrating previously disconnected knowledge. The concept of a "liminal state" — oscillating between understanding and confusion before crossing the threshold — captures the uncomfortable but necessary transitional period. For explanation design, the implication is that certain concepts deserve disproportionate investment because they unlock cascading understanding, and that the discomfort of liminality should be expected and supported rather than shortcut.

## The explore-then-tell sequence outperforms traditional instruction for understanding

Manu Kapur's productive failure research (2008–2024) demonstrates that struggling with a problem before receiving instruction enhances learning compared to receiving instruction first. A meta-analysis of 95 comparisons over 15 years (Sinha & Kapur, 2021) found that productive failure students outperformed direct-instruction students on conceptual understanding and transfer with **d = 0.36**, reaching **d = 0.58** with high design fidelity — approximately three times the effect of a good teacher. The mechanism involves four processes: activation and differentiation of prior knowledge, attention to critical conceptual features, explanation and elaboration, and organization of features into targeted concepts. Students who struggle first notice what they don't know, making subsequent instruction meaningful rather than inert.

Schwartz and Bransford's (1998) "preparation for future learning" paradigm reveals why timing matters so profoundly. Their landmark finding: explicit instruction is effective **only if learners have been prepared with appropriate experiences**. Their "inventing to prepare for learning" studies showed that students who attempted to invent solutions using contrasting cases — even failing entirely — subsequently learned more deeply from the same lecture that taught other students less. A critical methodological insight from Schwartz and Martin (2004): the two approaches looked identical on standard tests but diverged dramatically on assessments that included learning resources, revealing that invention prepared students to **learn from new information** — a capacity invisible to conventional measurement.

Loibl, Roll, and Rummel's (2017) reconciliation of the explore-then-tell debate identified the crucial boundary condition: problem-solving before instruction fosters learning **only when specific design features are implemented** — namely contrasting cases during problem-solving or building instruction on student solutions during the instruction phase. Simply delaying instruction without these features provides no benefit. This resolves the apparent contradiction with Kirschner, Sweller, and Clark's (2006) defense of direct instruction: the two camps address different learning outcomes (procedural knowledge favors instruction-first; conceptual understanding and transfer favor explore-then-tell) and different measurement approaches.

Ference Marton's variation theory contributes a complementary principle: **"without variation there is no discernment."** Learning requires experiencing what varies against what stays constant. Marton and Pang (2006, 2013) established that **seeing differences precedes seeing sameness** — it is more effective to start with contrasting examples that are totally different before showing examples that are similar. The theory prescribes four ordered patterns: contrast (is vs. isn't), separation (vary the target feature while holding everything else constant), generalization (hold the target constant while varying everything else), and fusion (vary multiple features simultaneously). For example selection, this means each example should systematically vary one critical feature against an invariant background, and non-examples are essential because they define what the concept is not.

## Expert explainers build knowledge rather than tell it

Roscoe and Chi's (2007) research on tutoring establishes the fundamental cognitive distinction between effective and ineffective explanation. **Knowledge-telling** — summarizing, restating, revealing answers with little elaboration — is information delivery where the explainer retrieves and transmits pre-existing knowledge without transforming it. **Knowledge-building** — monitoring understanding, generating inferences, constructing new connections — involves reflective self-monitoring and elaboration. The devastating finding: even trained peer tutors exhibit a **pervasive knowledge-telling bias**, defaulting to delivery rather than development. The few tutors who spontaneously engaged in knowledge-building were distinguished by their metacognitive self-monitoring — making overt statements like "I'm confused" or "this is tricky" — and showed significantly higher post-test scores themselves.

Chi's ICAP framework (Chi & Wylie, 2014) formalizes the hierarchy: Interactive > Constructive > Active > Passive. The critical gulf lies **between Active and Constructive modes** — the shift from manipulating existing knowledge (highlighting, repeating, restating) to generating new knowledge (self-explaining, drawing inferences, making connections). This is the cognitive boundary between telling and genuine explanation. Explanation becomes generative when it requires the receiver to make inferences not explicitly stated, integrate new with prior knowledge, monitor their own comprehension, and resolve impasses through reasoning.

The self-explanation effect (Chi, Bassok et al., 1989; Chi et al., 1994) reveals the mechanism underlying this divide. "Good" students studying worked physics examples generated many self-explanations — refining conditions, relating actions to principles — **guided by accurate monitoring of their own understanding**. "Poor" students monitored inaccurately and generated insufficient self-explanations. Crucially, prior knowledge did not explain the difference; the act of self-explaining itself fostered learning. Chi identified a dual-process mechanism: self-explanation both fills inferential gaps in incomplete texts and repairs flawed mental models when contradictions are encountered. For explanation design, the implication is that strategic gaps and productive contradictions in an explanation can trigger self-explanation in the listener — but only if the listener has enough knowledge and time to attempt the inference.

Lombrozo's research on explanatory preferences (2007, 2016) adds important nuance about what makes an explanation satisfying versus effective — and how these can diverge. People overwhelmingly prefer **simpler explanations** (fewer independent causes), changing preference only when complex explanations are at least ten times more probable. Simplicity functions as a heuristic cue to probability, but it can bias learners toward oversimplified models. People also prefer causal and mechanistic explanations over correlational descriptions, and explanations that unify multiple effects under a single cause (the "scope" virtue). An emerging finding from Vrantsidis and Lombrozo (2022) suggests the **complexity-matching hypothesis**: people believe a satisfying explanation should be as complex as the event being explained, suggesting explanatory virtues are context-dependent rather than fixed.

## Discourse coherence runs on causal chains, and audio amplifies every structural flaw

Van den Broek's Landscape Model (1999) describes comprehension as a cyclical process where concepts fluctuate dynamically in activation, drawing on four sources: currently processed text, immediately preceding content, earlier content reactivated from episodic memory, and background knowledge. The model introduces **standards of coherence** — readers and listeners maintain a threshold of understanding and exert strategic retrieval effort only when comprehension drops below this threshold. In audio, where the listener cannot control pace, the consequence is stark: if coherence breaks and the listener cannot strategically reactivate earlier information (because the audio has moved on), the coherence break may never be repaired.

Trabasso and van den Broek's causal network analysis (1985) established that two variables dominate memory for narrative: **causal chain membership** (whether an event sits on the main cause-effect backbone) and **causal connectivity** (how many connections an event has to other events). Events off the causal chain — dead ends — are recalled poorly and judged unimportant regardless of other features. This means explanation content should be organized around a clear causal backbone, with every element either on the chain or explicitly connected to it.

Graesser, Singer, and Trabasso's constructionist theory (1994) specifies which inferences listeners generate automatically and which they do not. **Backward (explanatory) inferences** — causal antecedents, bridging inferences between adjacent sentences, character goals — are generated routinely. **Forward (predictive) inferences** and elaborative inferences are **not generated routinely** unless highly constrained. This asymmetry has a direct design implication: explanations should not assume listeners will fill in forward gaps or generate elaborations. Causal connections looking backward must be made explicit; forward connections require either explicit statement or deliberate prompting.

The distinction between local and global coherence reveals a differential vulnerability. Rogalski et al. (2010) found that **global coherence strongly correlates with cognitive function** (sustained attention, processing speed) while local coherence does not — and under dual-task conditions, global coherence is disrupted first. Neuroimaging (Lerner et al., 2013) identified three distinct functional networks: two supporting global integration and one for local integration. In extended spoken explanation, the global thread is what breaks first. Explicit signposting, periodic returns to the main argument, and hierarchical structure become not stylistic choices but cognitive necessities.

The **expertise reversal effect** (Kalyuga et al., 2003) means the same explanation structure works differently depending on listener knowledge. Instructional supports effective for novices — worked examples, integrated diagrams, step-by-step scaffolding — become **counterproductive for experts** because they constitute redundant information that must be reconciled with existing internal schemas, imposing additional working memory load. Kalyuga (2007) specifically notes that audio explanations may become redundant for experienced learners. This is not a gradual decline in benefit but can be a full crossover interaction: the same design feature that helps novices actively harms experts.

A meta-analysis by Clinton-Lisell found that reading outperforms listening specifically for **inferential and general comprehension** (g = 0.36) but not for literal comprehension (g = −0.01), and the reading advantage disappears when reading is experimenter-paced rather than self-paced. The implication: audio's disadvantage lies specifically in deeper processing that benefits from re-reading — precisely the kind of processing that genuine understanding requires. This places greater burden on audio explanations to achieve first-pass encoding quality, since there is no second pass.

## Epistemic emotions are functional cognitive states, not mere byproducts

D'Mello and Graesser (2014) identified confusion as the **"affective signature" of cognitive disequilibrium** — a distinct epistemic emotion triggered by contradictions, anomalies, and discrepancies. Their finding that confusion was the **only individual emotion that significantly predicted learning** (positively) overturns the intuition that confusion signals failure. The mechanism: confusion triggers the same disequilibrium-resolution cycle that drives conceptual change. But the finding carries a critical boundary condition: confusion must be **resolvable**. When learners cannot resolve confusion, it becomes destructive — they dismiss contradictory information through Chinn and Brewer's non-change responses.

Jordan Litman's distinction between **I-type (interest) and D-type (deprivation) epistemic curiosity** reveals that not all curiosity is created equal. I-type curiosity is the intrinsic pleasure of discovering new ideas — driven by joyous exploration, associated with positive affect and broad engagement. D-type curiosity is the aversive feeling of not knowing — driven by need to close a specific knowledge gap, associated with targeted information-seeking and a stronger motive for knowledge acquisition. Litman maps these onto Berridge's neuroscience of reward: I-type corresponds to the opioid-mediated "liking" system, D-type to the dopamine-mediated "wanting" system. An explanation should deploy both: D-type to create the felt urgency of a specific gap ("There's a reason this fails, and I'll explain in a moment"), I-type to sustain the pleasure of discovery through novelty and unexpected connections.

Vogl et al. (2019, 2020) found that **surprise, curiosity, and confusion are all triggered by cognitive incongruity** — specifically by high-confidence errors (believing you're right when you're wrong). This connects directly to the hypercorrection effect: the emotional experience of surprise at being wrong is not merely affective but drives enhanced encoding. The productive emotional sequence emerging from this research is: **surprise** (attention capture) → **curiosity** (motivation to explore) → **confusion** (deep processing) → **resolution** (learning and delight). Designing this sequence deliberately into an explanation would mean: present something that violates expectations, make the listener care about the violation, let them sit with the dissonance briefly, then resolve it.

The metacognitive dimension is equally crucial. Glenberg, Wilkinson, and Epstein's (1982) **"illusion of knowing"** describes the systematic overestimation of one's own comprehension — and it operates through a devastating mechanism: processing fluency is misinterpreted as understanding. Koriat's accessibility model (1995) specifies that feeling-of-knowing judgments are based on the ease of accessing any information about a topic, not on the quality or completeness of that information. A well-structured, fluent explanation can therefore mask comprehension failures by generating high processing fluency. Rozenblit and Keil's illusion of explanatory depth (2002) is strongest for **explanatory knowledge** with visible components — precisely the kind of knowledge that spoken explanation typically targets. The only reliable intervention: requiring the listener to generate their own explanation, which confronts them with the gaps in their knowledge.

## Embodied language activates the machinery of physical experience

Barsalou's perceptual symbol systems theory (1999) proposes that understanding a concept involves reactivating the sensory-motor states originally experienced when encountering that concept. The evidence is now substantial for concrete language: Hauk et al. (2004) showed that reading action verbs like "kick" produces somatotopic activation in motor cortex corresponding to the relevant body part. Glenberg and Kaschak (2002) demonstrated the action-sentence compatibility effect — comprehending sentences about actions interferes with actual movements in incompatible directions. Matlock (2004) found that participants read about traversing a 400-mile desert slower than a 30-mile desert, suggesting language triggers genuine spatial simulation.

For explanation design, this means that **concrete, action-rich, sensory language** is not merely more vivid — it activates different and additional neural processing systems. When Feynman described physics with full-body movement and sound effects, he was not merely being theatrical; he was recruiting his audience's sensorimotor simulation systems to build richer, more deeply encoded mental representations. The practical challenge is that abstract concepts — often the very targets of explanation — show weaker and more debated embodiment effects. The resolution may be metaphorical grounding: Lakoff and Johnson (1980) established that abstract concepts are systematically structured through bodily metaphor ("grasping" an idea, "seeing" the point), and these metaphorical mappings appear to activate sensorimotor schemas, though replication concerns remain (a p-curve analysis of TMS studies found the evidence base potentially underpowered).

## What remains genuinely unknown

Several cross-domain connections have not been made in the existing literature and represent genuine opportunities.

**Event segmentation applied to explanation architecture.** Zacks's work on event boundaries has been studied extensively in narrative and film but has not been systematically applied to instructional explanation. The finding that memory is enhanced at event boundaries, combined with the prediction-error triggering mechanism, suggests a principled basis for where to place structural breaks in an explanation — and more importantly, where not to place them.

**The curiosity vortex as a design primitive.** Gruber and Ranganath's (2014) finding that dopaminergic curiosity states enhance memory for all temporally adjacent information has transformative implications for explanation sequencing that have barely been explored. If triggering D-type curiosity before a passage creates a neurochemical window of enhanced encoding for everything in that window — including material only incidentally related to the curiosity trigger — then curiosity management becomes the highest-leverage design variable available.

**Hypercorrection as an active explanation strategy.** The finding that high-confidence errors are corrected more durably than low-confidence errors has been studied in laboratory settings but not systematically incorporated into explanation design. Combined with Metcalfe and Miele's (2014) finding that a post-feedback test protects against error return, this suggests a powerful three-step pattern: elicit the confident error, correct it with surprising feedback, then immediately prompt the listener to rehearse the correction.

**Predictive processing as a unified framework for explanation.** Clark and Friston's predictive processing account has been applied to perception, action, and psychopathology, but its implications for explanation design remain largely unexplored in the literature. The framework's central claim — that an explanation that is 100% predictable generates zero prediction error and therefore zero learning — provides a principled account of why "too clear" explanations fail, and why strategic difficulty works when it does (and fails when it doesn't): difficulties are desirable precisely when they generate prediction errors in the right neural systems, and undesirable when they generate prediction error in irrelevant systems (e.g., deciphering poor audio rather than processing concepts).

**The coherence-correspondence tension in real-time listening.** Johnson-Laird's distinction between coherence (internal consistency) and correspondence (match with reality) in mental models has not been applied to the dynamics of explanation reception. Listeners naturally prioritize coherence — building internally consistent models — sometimes at the expense of correspondence with reality. An explanation that is internally coherent but factually simplified may produce confident but wrong understanding. This tension suggests that effective explanation needs to build correspondence (accuracy) through coherence (internal logic), and that moments where these goals conflict deserve explicit acknowledgment.

**Reading-to-listening transfer gaps.** The meta-analytic finding that reading's advantage over listening is specifically concentrated in inferential comprehension — the kind that requires re-processing — suggests that many findings from text comprehension research may overestimate what listeners can achieve in real time. Systematic investigation of which established comprehension findings do and don't transfer to the auditory modality represents a significant gap in the literature, one that matters for anyone designing explanations to be heard rather than read.

## Conclusion: toward a cognitive science of explanation design

The research synthesized here converges on a picture of understanding that is more violent and discontinuous than the folk model of knowledge transfer suggests. Understanding is not information flowing from explainer to listener; it is the listener's predictive model breaking and being rebuilt. The explainer's task is not to transmit but to engineer productive failure in the listener's mental model — to create the precise conditions under which the model breaks in exactly the right place, and the right new structure becomes available to replace it.

The deepest actionable insight across this literature is the **prediction-error principle**: the brain updates its models specifically at points of calibrated surprise — not too small (no learning signal), not too large (overwhelm and dismissal). This unifies productive failure, the hypercorrection effect, event segmentation, epistemic emotions, and curiosity research under a single computational principle. The practical architecture this suggests is: establish a predictive framework the listener can use to generate expectations, then violate those expectations at exactly the moments where the model needs updating, with the violation delivered inside a dopaminergic curiosity window that enhances encoding of the correction.

The second meta-finding is the **generative imperative**: understanding requires the listener to construct, not receive. Self-explanation, the generation effect, the pretesting effect, and Chi's ICAP hierarchy all converge on the same mechanism. An explanation that does all the cognitive work for the listener — that achieves maximum clarity and smoothness — may paradoxically produce the least understanding, because it generates no prediction errors, triggers no self-explanation, and provides no opportunity for knowledge-building. The optimal explanation is one that is clear enough to maintain coherence but incomplete enough to demand inference — a design tension that cannot be resolved by formula but requires continuous calibration to the listener's knowledge state, which itself shifts throughout the explanation.

The most well-established findings in this synthesis are: the neural correlates of insight (Beeman & Kounios), the Construction-Integration model's three levels (Kintsch), structure mapping theory (Gentner), Chinn and Brewer's taxonomy of non-change responses, the self-explanation effect (Chi), productive failure (Kapur), the expertise reversal effect (Kalyuga), causal chain dominance in memory (Trabasso & van den Broek), and the illusion of explanatory depth (Rozenblit & Keil). The most promising emerging areas are: event segmentation applied to explanation architecture, the curiosity vortex as a design primitive, predictive processing as a unified framework, and the hypercorrection effect as an active strategy. The most contested are: the scope of embodied language effects, the generalizability of concreteness fading beyond mathematics, and whether perceptual disfluency produces any genuine learning benefits (meta-analyses suggest not, but conceptual disfluency may). The largest genuine unknown is how these mechanisms interact in real-time extended listening — nearly all the research examines these processes in isolation, and the combinatorics of their interaction in a 20- or 60-minute explanation remain almost entirely unexplored.
